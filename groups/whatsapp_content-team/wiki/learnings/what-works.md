## Patterns — What These Articles Share

### 1. One Question Answered Exhaustively

Every 95+ article has a single, specific focus and answers it completely. No article tries to cover two topics. The reader comes with one question and leaves with a definitive answer.

- "How many calories?" — answered by weight and intensity in comprehensive tables
- "Which muscles?" — answered by muscle group with diagram
- "What to write to a friend?" — answered for 11 types of friendship

The differentiator vs. low-scoring articles: no padding to hit a word count. Every section earns its place.

### 2. Organised by a Natural Sub-Category

High-scoring articles don't present a flat list. They group content by the dimension the reader actually uses to navigate:

- **Relationship type** — friend, boss, boyfriend, grandson (different tone per relationship)
- **Milestone / age** — 13th, 18th, 40th (different content per life stage)
- **Budget / price tier** — what to get for under $30, $30-$75, $75+
- **Vibe / style** — glamorous, nostalgic, low-key, outdoor, activity-based
- **Intensity / weight** — calorie tables structured by these axes

This structure makes the article scannable AND extractable by AI engines, which can pull the relevant sub-section for a specific query.

### 3. Specific, Concrete Examples — Not "Some Ideas"

The difference between 95+ and 80-90:

- 99/100 Birthday Letters: **15 named templates**, each with a distinct scenario and voice
- 98/100 18th Birthday: **30+ specific ideas**, not "try a rooftop bar or maybe a dinner"
- 99/100 Compact Bikes: **7 specific bikes reviewed** with real specs, not a generic "look for these features"
- 100/100 Calories: **tables with actual numbers**, not "it depends on intensity"

Specificity is the primary GEO differentiator. AI answer engines extract concrete facts, not hedged generalities.

### 4. Named, Dated External Sources — Always Tier 1/2

Every 95+ article cites specific studies, not "research suggests":

- APA, NIH/PMC, Springer Nature, USGA, NRF, CNBC
- Sources are named in-text: "the [Source Name] ([Year]) found..."
- Minimum 2 Tier 1/2 sources per article
- Articles about health/fitness topics skew heavily Tier 1 (peer-reviewed journals)

The 98/100 18th Birthday article was docked only 2 points — E-E-A-T citations at 13/15 because only one Tier 1 source was available for that topic. That's the ceiling when topic limits sources.

### 5. Comparison Tables for Product and App Content

Equipment and app comparison articles perform best with structured tables:

- Zwift Alternatives: comparison matrix (features, price, platform compatibility)
- Compact Bikes: model specs (dimensions, weight, resistance type, price)
- Calories: weight × intensity grid

Tables make content extractable by AI engines and are the primary reason product/comparison articles score so consistently.

### 6. Visual Summary Infographic Placed After TL;DR

Every 95+ article has a Nano Banana infographic that summarises the article's structure visually:

- Muscles article: anatomical body diagram with primary/secondary muscles highlighted
- Calories article: calorie burn chart by intensity
- 13th Birthday: "By Vibe" poster (4 vibe categories × 4-5 ideas)
- Party Themes: themed grid (vibe × theme examples)
- Languages: world map with 12 languages in native script

Placement: **immediately after TL;DR**, not at the bottom. This is a hard rule — infographics placed at the bottom are flagged for repositioning.

### 7. Voice Holds Throughout — No Editorial Drift

Articles that score highest don't drift into "content editor voice" mid-article. The voice check at the end of Mindy's pass:

- Tracey's voice: warm, personal, conversational — reads like a text from a knowledgeable friend
- Adam's voice: direct, practical, slightly self-deprecating — reads like advice from a training partner
- No section intros announcing what the section covers
- No closing "In summary" or "In conclusion" — always a specific next step or takeaway

Voice drift is the primary cause of scores dropping from 95+ to 85-90. Articles that pass Prism clean typically required fewer Mindy revision cycles.

### 8. First-Attempt or Single-Revision Passes

The 100/100 and 99/100 articles all passed Prism on the first or second attempt. When revision cycles stack up (3+), the final score rarely exceeds 92-93. First-attempt passes correlate with:

- Quill brief being specific and well-scoped
- Scout sourcing quality external links at research stage
- Correct intent type assigned at brief creation (pillar-page vs. listicle vs. explainer)

## Patterns to Avoid (from Low-Scoring Articles)

- Generic images not matching section subject — flagged in multiple Apr 4 articles; images replaced post-publish
- Opening paragraphs too long — flagged by Adam on post 199 (Tracey)
- Flat lists without sub-category organisation — listicles with no grouping logic
- Hedged external citations — "experts say" without naming or dating the source
- Infographics at the bottom of the article — must be immediately after TL;DR
- Internal link anchor text that reads like navigation ("see our guide to X") — must read like Tracey or Adam naturally recommending something
